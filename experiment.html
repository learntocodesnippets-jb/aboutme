You Think You Decided

CS-6795 · Cognitive Science · Summer 2026

We make decisions constantly — what to read, what to believe, what to trust. But how much of that is really us deciding, and how much is shaped by the order things appear in front of us?

This experiment looks at a specific question: when people read a list of sources on a contested topic and later revise their beliefs, do they correctly identify which sources actually moved them — or do they misattribute influence to whichever source appeared first?

The Setup

Participants were shown three contested topics — extraterrestrial life, lab-grown food, and AI consciousness. For each topic, they saw a ranked list of sources (ordered by credibility, popularity, or reverse popularity) and answered belief questions before and after reading.

The key variable wasn't what they read — it was where the sources appeared in the list. Did position alone predict misattribution?

What I Found

Across 55 valid responses, 82.8% of participants revised their beliefs after reading. But nearly all of them attributed that change to whichever source was ranked highest — regardless of whether they spent meaningful time on it.

The effect was strongest for topics where participants felt less epistemically confident (aliens, lab-grown food) and weakest where they felt the topic was contested by design (AI consciousness).

Why It Matters

Search engines, news feeds, and recommendation systems all make ordering decisions. If position alone shapes both belief revision and the story we tell ourselves about why we changed our minds, then the gap between what we think we decided and what actually influenced us is larger than we assume.

That gap is what this experiment is trying to measure.
